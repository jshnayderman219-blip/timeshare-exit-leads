How to Legally Exit Your Timeshare

A practical guide for owners who want out — and want it done right.

📊 The Reality: Over 9 million U.S. households own a timeshare. Many want out but don't know where to start. The exit industry is filled with scams, misleading promises, and conflicting advice — which is why we wrote this guide.

Why Timeshare Exit Is So Complicated

If getting out were easy, you would've done it already. Here's why most owners get stuck:

  • Resorts make the process deliberately confusing. Different resorts, different rules, different states, different contract types. What works for a Wyndham owner in Florida doesn't work for a Hilton owner in Nevada.
  • The resale market is dead. Your timeshare is worth 0–10% of what you paid. Listing it for sale usually leads nowhere — or worse, to companies that charge you to "market" it with no results.
  • Exit companies are a minefield. The FTC actively warns about the timeshare exit industry. Many companies charge $3,000–$10,000 upfront and deliver nothing.
  • Every situation is different. Are you current on payments? Behind? Is the loan paid off? What state is the property in? Did the salesperson make promises they didn't keep? All of this changes your options.

The Exit Options (Ranked by Real-World Effectiveness)

1. Professional Exit Representation

For most owners, the highest-percentage path is having a professional handle the exit from start to finish. A legitimate exit professional:

  • Evaluates your specific contract, resort, and state laws
  • Identifies the exit path that actually applies to YOUR situation
  • Handles all communication and paperwork with the resort
  • Works with timeshare attorneys when legal cancellation is needed
  • Gives you a timeline and terms in writing — not empty promises

What to expect: Legitimate services charge a flat fee (typically paid in stages — not all upfront), have a physical business address, and will tell you honestly if your situation has no viable exit path.

⚠️ How to Spot an Exit Scam:
The FTC warns that many exit companies are fraudulent. Red flags: upfront fees over $1,500 before any work is done, "100% guarantee" claims, high-pressure sales tactics, refusal to put terms in writing, no physical office address, or claiming they can cancel ANY timeshare. Legitimate professionals are upfront about what's possible — and what isn't.

2. Transfer or Give Away

If your timeshare has some value (desirable resort, prime week), transferring it to a new owner may work — but it's harder than it sounds:

  • List on resale marketplaces (expect to recover pennies on the dollar)
  • Offer to pay the new owner's first year of maintenance fees as incentive
  • Use a licensed timeshare closing company for the paperwork (not a "marketing" company)
  • Be prepared for a long wait — even free timeshares are hard to give away

Warning: Do not pay anyone an upfront fee to "list" or "market" your timeshare. You can list it yourself for free on multiple platforms.

3. Legal Cancellation

Under certain circumstances, a timeshare contract can be legally cancelled:

  • Misrepresentation at sale: If the salesperson lied about costs, availability, rental income, or investment value, you may have grounds for cancellation.
  • Cooling-off period: Most states give you 3–10 days to cancel after signing. If you're past this window, this doesn't apply.
  • State-specific protections: Some states have stronger consumer protection laws for timeshare contracts.

This path requires an attorney familiar with timeshare law. It's not a DIY option — but a legitimate professional can tell you in the first consultation whether you have a case.

4. Stop Paying (Understand the Full Consequences)

Stopping maintenance fee payments triggers foreclosure. Before considering this, understand:

  • Credit score impact — the resort reports to credit bureaus
  • Collections — unpaid fees are sent to debt collectors
  • Foreclosure — the resort takes back the timeshare through legal action
  • Deficiency judgments — in some states, the resort can sue you for the remaining balance
  • Some states protect owners (Florida, California have anti-deficiency laws) — but this varies widely

Never stop paying without consulting a timeshare attorney first. What looks like a shortcut can become a much bigger problem.

  • DIY takes months of research. Resort policies change. State laws vary. One wrong step and you're back at square one — or worse.
